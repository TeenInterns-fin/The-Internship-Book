Finding Your First Internship

üë§ Author: Aditi
üé® Illustrator: Anya

Finding your first internship can feel overwhelming. Where do you even start? How do you find opportunities that are open to high school students? What skills do you need to showcase? We've been there too, and we're here to break it down into simple, actionable steps that anyone can follow.

This guide expands on the content in Chapter 3 of our book with additional resources, detailed examples, and a video walkthrough of the application process.

Know What You're Looking For

Before diving into applications, take some time to reflect on your interests, skills, and goals. Ask yourself:

  • What fields or industries excite me?
  • What skills do I want to develop?
  • How much time can I commit each week?
  • Do I need a remote opportunity or can I commute?
  • Am I looking for a paid position or is unpaid acceptable?

Having clarity on these points will help you focus your search and identify opportunities that are a good fit for you.

Research Potential Opportunities

Now it's time to find where the internships are hiding! Here are the best places to look:

  • School Resources: Talk to your guidance counselor, career center, or teachers who may know of opportunities.
  • Online Platforms: Sites like LinkedIn, Indeed, and specialized teen internship platforms like Teen Interns.
  • Company Websites: Many organizations have dedicated "Careers" pages with internship listings.
  • Professional Networks: Ask family friends, relatives, or neighbors who work in fields you're interested in.
  • Social Media: Follow companies and organizations on platforms like Instagram and Twitter, where they often announce opportunities.

Prepare Your Application Materials

Most internship applications will require some or all of the following:

  • Resume: Even if you don't have work experience, you can highlight school projects, volunteer work, extracurricular activities, and relevant skills.
  • Cover Letter: A personalized introduction explaining why you're interested in the position and what you bring to the table.
  • Portfolio: For creative fields, compile examples of your work (writing samples, design projects, coding examples, etc.)
  • References: Teachers, club advisors, or volunteer supervisors who can speak to your skills and character.

Pro tip: Customize your materials for each application to highlight relevant skills and experiences!

Submit Strong Applications

When applying for internships, quality matters more than quantity. Focus on submitting thoughtful applications rather than applying to dozens of positions with generic materials.

  • Read the job description carefully and address specific requirements.
  • Research the organization before applying to understand their mission and values.
  • Follow all application instructions precisely.
  • Proofread everything‚Äîtypos and grammatical errors can disqualify an otherwise strong application.
  • Submit your application before the deadline (early is even better!).

Prepare for Interviews

If your application impresses, you'll likely be invited for an interview. Here's how to prepare:

  • Research common interview questions and practice your responses.
  • Prepare concrete examples that demonstrate your skills and character.
  • Dress professionally, even for virtual interviews.
  • Prepare thoughtful questions to ask the interviewer.
  • For virtual interviews, test your technology in advance and find a quiet, well-lit location.

Remember: Interviews are a two-way street. While they're evaluating you, you're also determining if the opportunity is right for you.

Common Challenges and How to Overcome Them

Finding your first internship isn't always smooth sailing. Here are some common challenges and how to navigate them:

  • Limited Experience: Focus on transferable skills from school projects, volunteer work, or extracurricular activities.
  • Age Restrictions: Look for programs specifically designed for high school students or consider creating your own opportunity (more on this below).
  • Competitive Field: Develop a unique angle or skill that sets you apart, and consider starting with smaller organizations where there may be less competition.
  • Lack of Connections: Start building your network by attending career fairs, joining relevant clubs, and reaching out to professionals for informational interviews.

Creating Your Own Opportunity

Can't find an existing internship that fits? Consider creating your own! Here's how:

  1. Identify organizations you're interested in, even if they don't advertise internships.
  2. Research their needs and challenges, and think about how you could help.
  3. Craft a compelling pitch that explains what you could offer and what you hope to learn.
  4. Reach out directly to the appropriate person with your proposal.
  5. Be flexible with terms‚Äîyou might start as a volunteer and transition to a more formal role.

Many successful internships begin this way, and it shows initiative that impresses future employers!
